The friendliest read of Vance's Iowa swing is that primary voters, even in a soft early state, still want a story they can tell themselves about the next four years. Vance is auditioning for the role of "competent restoration" — a Trump apprentice who can keep the coalition together while sanding off the chaos. Iowa Republicans, per Politico's reporting, were polite. They were also clear about what they actually wanted: someone who could fix the economy.

That is the trap.

The Iran conflict is now in its sixth month. The most recent NPR-Marist poll has Trump's approval underwater by double digits, with 80% of respondents reporting that gas prices are straining their household budgets. Internal Republican polling, per the Politico piece, mirrors the public numbers — Vance's favorables track Trump's almost exactly, with a meaningful drag in suburban districts that historically decide primaries.

This is the structural problem with being the chosen heir: you inherit the house, the deed, and the structural damage. There is no clean version of "Trumpism without Trump" because Trumpism IS Trump's specific personality married to a coalition that re-formed around it. Vance can soften the edges, but he can't change the brand position. By 2028, the question primary voters will be asking is not "Who's most loyal?" but "Who's most electable?" — and electability, in Republican primaries that have to win general elections, is a polling question.

Three things to watch:

- **The Iowa caucus straw polls in early 2027.** If Vance is running below 35% with Trump still in office, he has a real problem with Republican voters who are reading the same numbers we are.
- **Whether a "next-generation" challenger emerges from the governor class.** Glenn Youngkin, Brian Kemp, or someone like them — running as the version of the GOP that doesn't carry the federal economic record. The lane exists because Vance can't run away from his own boss.
- **What happens in the 2026 midterms.** A meaningful Democratic wave changes the read on every 2028 calculation. A House wave especially — it would re-set the institutional power balance and reframe the strategist conversation.

The historical comparison isn't recent. It's George H.W. Bush in 1988, who inherited a popular two-term president and still had to spend the entire general election proving he wasn't running for "Reagan's third term." Bush won. He also lost the next cycle. That is what the heir-apparent track looks like even on the success path.

Vance gets one shot at this. He can spend the next 18 months trying to define himself as something other than Trump's continuation — which alienates the coalition that made him — or he can lean fully in, and run on whatever Trump's record actually is by then. The first path requires courage Vance has not shown. The second requires the economy to recover, which is a question of luck more than strategy.

Either way, Iowa was the easy stop.
