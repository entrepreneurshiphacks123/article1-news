    The Congressional Budget Office finds that 60 percent of that figure —
    roughly $720 billion — would go toward space-based interceptors that do not
    yet exist as operational technology. The last time a president asked
    Congress to fund a missile defense architecture built on unproven space
    systems, it was Reagan's Strategic Defense Initiative in 1983; Congress
    never appropriated the full request and the program spent decades as a
    budget negotiation rather than a deployed shield. The constitutional
    question is the same now as it was then: Article I gives the power of the
    purse to the legislature, and a $1.2 trillion commitment to hardware that
    exists mainly in proposal form is precisely the kind of open-ended executive
    wish list that appropriations authority was designed to discipline.
