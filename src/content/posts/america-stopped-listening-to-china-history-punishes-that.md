  A new analysis in The Hill argues that the United States has sharply curtailed
  the academic exchanges, business presence, and research engagement that once
  gave American policymakers genuine insight into Chinese society, economics,
  and intent — and that the resulting knowledge gap is now a strategic
  liability.


  The pattern has precedent. In the 1950s, McCarthyite purges drove out the
  State Department's China hands — the diplomats and scholars who actually read,
  spoke, and traveled in Asia. Their absence meant Washington went into Korea
  and Vietnam nearly blind to how Beijing calculated risk and commitment. The
  cost was measured in lives and decades.


  Self-imposed ignorance is not strength policy; it is fear policy dressed in
  the language of security. A republic that cannot see its rivals clearly cannot
  govern itself wisely in relation to them.
